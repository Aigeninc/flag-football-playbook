## OFFENSE

### Best Plays for 5v5 Youth (Specific Formations & Designs)

#### Split Back Formation
- **Setup:** QB under center, two running backs ("A" and "B") split 5-7 yards behind, fingertips almost touching — that's how wide they stand
- **Core play — the fake handoff:** QB turns, one RB chomps down (fakes receiving) and follows "the yellow brick road" to the sideline, second RB takes real handoff immediately after and goes opposite direction
- **Teaching cue:** "Follow the yellow brick road" = run the long way to the sideline, not cutting inside. Place a line of cones to mark this path.
- **Coaching insight:** This reveals your best fakers. You need 2-3 kids who can sell the fake. Drill it as rapid reps: fake line vs. real-ball line switching back and forth.
- **Why it works at this age:** Defenders react to the first motion. Kids who haven't been coached default to the fake because it looks real when the RB "chomps down" with elbow up.

#### Tight Formation (T-Formation)
- **Setup:** Center and both RBs are foot-to-foot — literally touching feet. RBs are directly behind the center.
- **Core play:** RB goes between center and QB to take the handoff. Very tight space, so drill it specifically.
- **Fake variant:** Same formation, A fakes one way, B takes it the other.
- **Key drill for this:** Have kids line up in exact formation over and over so the spacing becomes automatic. Kids default to standing wrong when they haven't drilled it.

#### Trips Formation (3 receivers one side)
- Referenced as advanced option. For 5v5, use with 2 receivers left, 1 right or vice versa to flood a zone.

### Play Calling Strategy (When to Run What)

- **Open the game with your base play** — something they know cold. Build confidence first.
- **When defense is blitzing:** Use quick screen or direct snap to RB at the line. Get rid of the ball in under 2 seconds.
- **When defense is in zone coverage:** Use crossing routes to create conflicts — one receiver pulls the zone, another receiver fills the vacated space.
- **When up by a lot:** Use the slower, practice-dependent plays to build confidence in less athletic kids. Stakes are lower.
- **Coach D's rusher decision rule:** Only rush/blitz your fastest defender if it actually impacts the QB. If the QB has a hot read and gets a quick throw off every time, pull your rusher back into coverage. Don't give up the gap for nothing.

### How to Teach Routes to 8-10 Year Olds

**The cone method (Coach D):** Don't just tell them where to go — put cones on the field showing the exact path. Remove after a few reps when they have it. This eliminates the "I didn't know where to go" problem.

**Route teaching progression:**
1. Walk it through with cones marking each landmark
2. Run at 50% — learn the shape
3. Run at 75% — add timing
4. Full speed with QB
5. Add defender

**Simple routes that work for this age:**
- **Out route (5-7 yards, break to sideline):** Drill as the "jerk drill" — 5 yards up, 2 steps outside, break back inside to receive, tuck, turn upfield. NFL FLAG cue: "Good sell" — make the defender think you're going inside before breaking out.
- **Go route (straight deep):** Easiest to run, hardest to complete. Use against man coverage when you have a speedster.
- **In/drag route (5-7 yards, break inside):** Great against zone. QB looks the safety one way, hits the drag.
- **Comeback route:** Run 10 yards, stop, come back 2-3 yards to the ball. Hard for kids at first — teach the STOP.

**Catching mechanics (NFL FLAG):**
- Diamond/triangle with thumbs together for balls above the waist
- Pinkies together for balls at or below the waist — "pinkies down, low ball"
- No T-Rex arms — extend fully to meet the ball
- Gloves make everything easier and are highly recommended
- "Watch it in" — eyes on ball all the way into the hands before tucking

**QB throwing mechanics (NFL FLAG):**
- 2 fingers on laces or 1 — personal preference, but there should be a small pocket of air between the ball and the palm
- Thumb snaps DOWN to opposite hip on release — not over the top
- Hips rotate through — front of hips point toward target at release
- High release — ball up, release high, snap through
- 5-yards warmup before going deeper

### QB Development Tips for Kids

**Pocket awareness drill (NFL FLAG):**
- Center snaps, QB watches coach's hand signal pointing left or right
- Coach points multiple directions simulating pass rush movement
- On "release," QB throws to stationary WR on that side
- Add scramble: QB rolls out after reading, throws to WR who then runs for TD

**Key coaching cue:** "When you roll out, still get your steps and footwork right — don't just flail and throw."

**Teaching multiple QBs:** Coach D recommends rotating QBs through practice. Use the split-back fake drill: all they do is the turn-and-hand motion. Isolate just that skill with the QB.

**Practice structure for QB development:**
- QB station can run simultaneously while other kids do RB drills — keeps everyone moving
- Warm up at 5 yards, then 10, then route-specific throws
- Always end with success reps

## DEFENSE

### Zone vs. Man Concepts for Youth

**Coach D's recommendation for 5v5:**
- Run **zone** as your base. Man coverage requires athletically matched players; zone hides mismatches better.
- Basic zone: 2-3 or 1-3-1 formation. Front players contain sidelines, back players handle deep routes.
- **Communicate, communicate, communicate:** NFL FLAG coaches emphasize defenders yelling "out! out!" when a receiver runs an out route, "in! in!" for in routes. Safety calls "I got it!" to claim responsibility.

**Zone assignment communication cues (NFL FLAG):**
- DB covering a route calls out the route type out loud
- Safety calls "switch" when taking over a receiver from the corner
- Everyone says "me, me, me" loudly when a ball is in the air and they're going for it

**When to consider man coverage:**
- Only when your defenders are clearly faster/more athletic than the matching receiver
- Don't do pure man with beginners — they'll get burned on every play

### Teaching Flag Pulling Technique

**The fundamental problem Coach D identifies:** Kids default to grabbing low or swiping — they miss flags constantly. The fix: **pull from the handle, pull UP not across.**

**Coach D's 3-step technique:**
1. **Break down** — get as wide and low as possible ("get big"). Feels unnatural, must be drilled.
2. **Shuffle laterally** — don't plant and lunge, shuffle side-to-side, keep square to ball carrier. Watch the belly button and hips, not the head or ball (fakes happen with head/shoulders, hips don't lie).
3. **Pull from the handle at the top** — reach up, grab the handle where it attaches, yank it cleanly. This prevents slipping off the flag.

**Flag pulling specific drills:**

**Spinner Pull (Coach D) — 4-cone diamond, great for every practice:**
- 4 kids on cones with flags on, 1 defender in middle doing quick feet
- Kids are assigned numbers 1-4, but stand on any cone (randomize)
- One kid yells "ball!" — defender sprints to that cone, pulls flag with proper technique
- While defender is running, that kid rotates/spins their body to simulate a moving target
- Defender must break down and pull properly even as the target is moving
- Rotation: after all 4 pulls, defender rotates out, new person comes in
- **This drill has <15 seconds downtime per kid when running 2 stations**

**Gauntlet (Coach D / MOJO):**
- 1 ball carrier, several defenders spaced every 5 yards
- Defenders can ONLY move laterally — no forward/backward
- Ball carrier sprints straight through (Coach D: speed is your friend, juking slows you down and gives the defender time)
- Teaches defenders to square up and lateral shuffle; teaches offense that straight speed beats dancing
- **Key coaching cue:** "Follow the ball carrier's belly button, not their head"
- Multiple reps fast — coach yells "down, set, go" repeatedly
- Runner goes around outside after their run so the drill doesn't stop

**Zigzag drill (NFL FLAG):**
- People hold flags, one flag puller zigzags through pulling inside flag, outside flag, then both flags in sequence
- Pure muscle memory — "we're just working on that feel"

**Chase drill (NFL FLAG):**
- Puller creeps up on stationary person, pulls flag, then turns and runs
- That person must now run down the puller and pull THEIR flag
- Teaches pursuit angles and competitive intensity

**2v1 drill (NFL FLAG):**
- 2 defenders start backpedaling, run around cone
- When they come around the cone, coach calls "release!" — 1 ball carrier takes off
- 2 defenders must now work together to pull the flag
- Teaches angles and communication between defenders

**Know Your Leverage drill (NFL FLAG):**
- 5 cones each side, 2 defenders, 1 RB
- Coach assigns direction to RB (behind defense so they can't see)
- Defenders learn to "know their leverage" — which side to cut off based on the RB's release direction
- Coaching cue: "Who's got him? Who's got him?" — defenders must communicate out loud

### Defensive Formations for 5v5

**Coach D's 5v5 defense options:**

**2-3 formation:**
- 2 linebackers at 3 yards off line of scrimmage (one on each hash)
- 3 defensive backs: 2 corners at 7-10 yards, 1 safety at 10-15 yards
- Best for: balanced coverage, stopping both run and pass

**1-3-1 formation:**
- 1 linebacker (middle, 3 yards off ball) — your best flag puller
- 3 across at 7-10 yards
- 1 safety deep (15 yards) — your best athlete, reads the whole field
- Best for: when you have one dominant flag puller who can roam

**Linebacker contain rule ("Red Rover"):**
- Outside linebackers contain the sideline — get as wide as possible and "nobody gets by"
- These can be less athletic kids — their job is to funnel runners inward
- Tell them: "Practice getting big everywhere — kitchen, school, anywhere. Just get big and get in the way."
- This forces the ball carrier toward your better athletes inside

**Rusher/blitzer (middle linebacker):**
- Must be 7 yards off the ball in most youth leagues
- Line up offset from the center — to the right OR left, coming at the QB on an angle
- The "snowplow" technique: get to QB, go big and wide, don't just run past
- **Only rush when it's actually working.** If QB gets the ball off before you arrive, drop that rusher into coverage and cover the middle instead.

### How to Teach Defensive Reads to Kids

**Teach safeties the "field awareness" concept:**
- Look at the whole formation before the snap
- Identify: is it trips (3 receivers one side)? Which side am I covering?
- Read: what route is coming? Can I see the receiver's alignment giving it away?
- React: move before the ball is thrown, not after

**Defensive back stance (NFL FLAG):**
- Feet shoulder-width apart
- Knees bent, shoulders slightly over toes
- On balls of feet, slight bounce
- Hands up — ready to jam receiver at the line
- Coach cue: "If you're standing like Frankenstein, you'll get burned"

**Backpedal technique (NFL FLAG):**
- Backpedal while watching QB's eyes and the receiver simultaneously
- Every 3-5 yards, "open up your hip" — turn and run in the direction the receiver is going
- **Do NOT turn your whole body at once** — open the hip first, then the body follows
- Use peripheral vision — you don't want to lose track of landmarks while focused on receiver

**W Drill (NFL FLAG) — teaches all DB movement skills:**
- Cones set up in a W pattern
- DB backpedals to far cone, runs up around it, backpedals again, runs up and around
- Full speed through — "always go around the cone, not through it"
- Add: throw interception at the final break — DB says "me me me" and catches it
- Add level 2: after interception, running back appears, DB must pull flag

## PRACTICE STRUCTURE

### Optimal Practice Plan for 1-Hour Sessions

Based on Coach D's template + NFL FLAG structure:

**0:00-0:08 — Equipment check + Arrow warmup (8 min)**
- Kids arrive: cleats on, flags fitted, jersey tucked, mouthguard in
- Start Arrow drill IMMEDIATELY while you handle any equipment issues
- Arrow drill (Coach D): 4-cone diamond, ~5 yards apart
  - Start at back cone in defensive stance (knees bent, comfortable)
  - Run to front cone, circle it → shuffle to side cone → shuffle to far side cone → circle to front cone → backpedal to start
  - Level 1: just the footwork pattern
  - Level 2: add flag pullers at the side cones (ball carriers hold flags, defender pulls as they pass)
  - Level 3: end with a catch (throw ball at the end so they work hands too)

**0:08-0:20 — Flag pulling station (12 min)**
Run Spinner Pull drill (2 stations simultaneously — everyone working)
- 4-cone diamond per station, rotate through
- All kids get 2-3 turns as defender quickly
- If time: transition to Gauntlet drill for different stimulus

**0:20-0:35 — Skill stations, split group (15 min)**
Run simultaneously (solo coach hack: set up the drills so kids self-police)
- Station A (5-6 kids): WR catching drills — light catching → jerk drill → diamond technique practice
- Station B (3-4 kids): QB/RB handoff mechanics — yellow brick road drill, split back fake reps

**0:35-0:50 — Team plays practice (15 min)**
- Put plays out on the field with cones marking routes
- Walk through at 50% first
- Add speed: 75%, then full
- Rapid reps — as soon as one play finishes, immediately set up the next
- "Up tempo" — Coach D's principle: practice must be the speed of the game
- Use wristbands so you don't have to re-explain every play — kids look at wrist, go

**0:50-1:00 — Controlled scrimmage (10 min)**
- 5v5 live play with coaching pauses
- Call timeout to fix specific issues — max 30-second coaching pause
- End on a positive play — let a weaker player score last

### Warm-Up Routines That Teach Skills

**Arrow Drill (Coach D) — teaches defensive footwork:**
- 4 cones in diamond, ~5-10 yards between cones
- Pattern: back cone → sprint to front → shuffle to right → shuffle to left → sprint to front → backpedal to back
- Start in defensive cornerback stance each rep
- 2 lines, rapid reps — <15 seconds per kid
- It reveals your best athletes naturally (field awareness, body control)

**Captain's Coming (MOJO) — teaches cutting and listening:**
- Rectangular space with 4 named sides (North/South/East/West or Port/Starboard/etc.)
- Coach calls a side name → all kids sprint there immediately
- "Captain's coming!" → everyone freezes, stands at attention, salutes, yells back
- "Hit the deck!" → everyone drops
- "Raise the sails!" → custom action
- Teaches: cutting, change of direction, listening, reaction time — all in a fun game format

**What Time Is It, Big Bad Wolf (MOJO) — teaches evasive running:**
- Kids on one end, coach on other end with back turned
- Kids call "What time is it?" — coach calls a number (3 o'clock = 3 steps forward)
- Kids advance that many steps toward coach
- At "Dinner time!" — coach turns and chases, kids sprint back to start
- First tagged = new Big Bad Wolf
- Teaches pursuit angles (wolf), evasive running (kids)

**Obstacle Course Relay (MOJO) — teaches throw-catch under fatigue:**
- 2 lanes, 15 yards, 3 stations: 5 squats → 5 side hops → 5 high knees sets
- Run course, sprint back to 10-yard cone, throw to next person in line
- **Key:** final throw happens when they're already tired — game-realistic

### High-Participation Drills (Zero Lines)

**The golden rule from Coach D: "Rapid reps. Once one person's done, the next one goes. Your practice needs to be the speed of the game."**

**Drills structured for <15 seconds downtime:**

1. **Spinner Pull** — 2 diamond stations running simultaneously. 4 kids on cones per station, 1 defender. Rotates constantly. 8 kids always moving.

2. **Gauntlet** — all kids spread along 20-yard lane, ball carriers run one at a time but defenders ALL engage each run. Nobody stands still.

3. **Handoff Relay (MOJO)** — two lines 10 yards apart, ball gets exchanged as they cross, continuous motion. Everyone running always.

4. **Floor Is Lava (MOJO)** — pairs with tennis balls, everyone moving simultaneously to "islands" (cones), throwing/catching in constant motion. All kids engaged at once.

5. **Mascot Tag (MOJO)** — both teams moving simultaneously, story-based triggers. Everyone runs at the same time.

6. **Anchor Drill (MOJO)** — 3-yard square, 1 stationary anchor defender, runners take turns but anchor gets rapid reps. Rotate anchor between rounds.

7. **Reaction Flag Pull (NFL FLAG)** — 2 kids locked arm-in-arm, numbered 1 and 2. Coach calls a number — that person becomes defender, tries to pull partner's flag. Both engaged every rep.

8. **Run and Cut (NFL FLAG)** — 2 RBs run simultaneously, watch coach who points left or right mid-run, they cut that direction over the cone. Both going at once.

### Progression: Simple to Complex

**Week 1-2 — Foundation:**
- Equipment fitting, flag rules, basic positions
- Stance + shuffle (5 minutes every practice)
- Simple flag pull (stationary), introduce catching diamond
- Arrow warmup becomes routine so you don't have to explain it

**Week 3-4 — Skill Introduction:**
- Gauntlet (defense), Jerk route drill (offense)
- QB throwing mechanics (pocket awareness drill at 50%)
- 1 offensive play — split back formation, just the fake/hand-off mechanics, no routes yet
- Introduce "Follow the Yellow Brick Road" cone path

**Week 5-6 — Play Execution:**
- Add a second play
- Run plays vs. stationary defenders (walk-through)
- Spinner pull becomes standard warm-up
- Introduce zone defensive concepts verbally

**Week 7-8 — Live Application:**
- Full scrimmage with coaching pauses
- Identify fakers vs. speed players vs. flag pullers based on what you've seen
- Assign positions based on strength assessment
- Add one "surprise" play (reverse or misdirection)

## COACHING PHILOSOPHY

### Common Mistakes New Coaches Make

**1. Too much standing around (the #1 sin)**
From all 18 videos combined: kids in line = bad. Coach D and NFL FLAG both structure every drill so at least 2 kids are active simultaneously. The second there's a "line," you've lost attention.

**2. Explaining plays without cones on the field**
Coach D explicitly: "If I just explain it one time and then run the play in a game, they'll be like 'uh...' — they don't know where to stand." Put cones down showing each player's exact path every time.

**3. Trying to teach everything at once**
Coach D: "I have a plan but once you get on the field it goes crazy — so don't take yourself or this too seriously. When I relaxed and let go of everything I was trying to get done, it actually turned out a lot better."

**4. Running the blitz when it's not working**
Only rush the QB when it actually hurts the offense. If the QB is quick enough to beat the blitz with a short throw, pull your rusher into coverage.

**5. Putting weak players next to each other**
On defense: never stack two beginners. Coach D: "You never want weak and weak — otherwise they're going to dominate you on offense and you're done." Put a stronger player behind every weaker player.

**6. Practice at half-speed**
Coach D's key message: **"Up tempo."** Whatever speed your practice runs at, that's the speed kids will default to in games. If practice is slow and confused, games will be slow and confused. Rapid reps, no waiting.

**7. Letting kids practice wrong form**
"Don't let them get away with sloppy form — like this and this — uh-uh. Break down, shuffle, and follow the belly button." Setting soft standards early means the habit never develops.

**8. No wristbands / too much verbal re-explaining**
Wristbands = kids can look at their wrist and know the play. Eliminates the huddle time, keeps tempo high, and is more game-realistic.

### How to Keep 8-10 Year Olds Engaged

**Psychologically:**
- **Name everything.** "The Yellow Brick Road." "The Arrow." "Spinner Pull." "Up Tempo." Named concepts stick better than descriptions. Kids remember "Follow the Yellow Brick Road" three weeks later.
- **Keep it competitive without keeping score.** "First team to get all players through wins" is competitive. But nobody keeps a running season score on drills.
- **Celebrate specific things, not just outcomes.** "That fake was SO good, I had no idea who had the ball" > "Nice run."
- **Short attention spans → short explanations.** 30 seconds max to explain a drill, then do it. Adjust while it's running.

**Structurally:**
- Every drill should have a clear, observable "win" condition for each kid
- Rotate positions so everyone tries everything (Coach D explicitly says this about QB position)
- Give every kid the ball every practice — nobody is just blocking
- Faster kids in front of drill lines (if you must have a line) to minimize wait time for slower kids
- Two simultaneous stations whenever you have 8+ kids

**Specific techniques:**
- Use "word of the week" — Coach D introduces one concept per session with a memorable label
- Give kids jobs: "You're the cone counter this drill," "You're the ball chaser"
- Tie everything to a "why": "We do drills so your body does this automatically in the game — you don't have to think"

### Equal Playing Time Strategies

**Coach D's position philosophy:**
- All positions are important — frame it that way to kids AND parents
- Tell them once (if they want a specific position), listen, and try to accommodate — but you decide
- "Every position is critical to the success of the team — whether center, QB, defense front or back. Give your all because it all goes to a team victory."

**Practical rotation:**
- Identify 2-3 kids who can QB — rotate them through, not just the best thrower
- If a kid has a physical limitation, put them in a position where they can succeed defensively (linebacker contain — "get big, get in the way")
- Designate the "strong on both sides" rule: if a less athletic kid is at cornerback, put one of your best athletes at safety directly behind them

**Parent management:**
- Send an email before the season covering equipment recommendations (cleats, gloves, mouthguard)
- Explain position rotation philosophy upfront
- Show parents what a "faker" is — some kids who don't have speed are incredibly valuable as fakers and parents should understand that's a valued role

### Building Confidence in Less Athletic Kids

**The "Red Rover" linebacker role:**
- Assign kids who struggle athletically to outside linebacker — their only job is to get big and force runners inside
- "Practice getting big everywhere — in the kitchen, at school, anywhere. Just get as big as possible and get in the way."
- This is a genuinely important role, and it's achievable by any kid regardless of athleticism

**The faker role:**
- During the split-back drill, watch for kids who "chomp down" convincingly on fakes
- The best fakers are often NOT the fastest kids — they're smart, committed kids
- Coach D says he's always looking for 2-3 good fakers because they're valuable in many plays

**Skill-specific confidence building:**
- If a kid struggles catching, have them work with gloves — dramatically lowers failure rate
- If a kid struggles running routes, walk through with cones at 50% speed until the path is automatic
- Give less athletic kids a play designed for them — a crossing drag route where the QB can throw early, a short out where they don't need to beat anyone

**Attitude coaching:**
- "Don't be upset if you missed the flag or missed the ball. Have a positive attitude. You'll get it next time."
- Model this explicitly — when a defender misses a pull in the Zigzag drill: "That's all right. That's why we're here. We're working on it."
- Avoid "why didn't you..." language — use "next time, try..."

## GAME MANAGEMENT

### Timeout Strategy

- **Coach D: Use the no-huddle system.** With wristbands, kids can look at the play number on their wrist and get set without a huddle. This catches defenses that are still setting up.
- Save timeouts for: (1) after you've scored and need to get your defense ready, (2) when the offense is about to score and you need to make an adjustment, (3) when a kid is clearly confused about their assignment
- Don't burn timeouts for water breaks — build water into the play cadence ("after this next series, water")

### Halftime Adjustments

**Observation framework (Coach D):**
- What is their best player doing? Which side do they run to? Who is their QB's first look?
- Is your rusher actually hurting them, or is the QB beating the blitz every time? Adjust accordingly.
- Are they running to a weak corner every time? Your safety on that side needs to cheat toward that hash.

**Adjustment communication:**
- Keep it to ONE adjustment per half — kids can't process multiple changes
- Use a visual aid: draw it on the clipboard, show it, say it once, then rep it with 3 walk-throughs during warmup before second half
- "Word of the half" — give it a label. "This half, we're all about the snowplow on defense."

### Substitution Patterns

**For 9 kids, 5v5 format:**
- You have 4 subs — rotate in groups of 2 each possession
- Track who hasn't had the ball recently, not just who performs best
- Pre-assign who comes off on each drive so you're not making real-time decisions under pressure
- Consider fixed-role subs: your weakest 2 players always come in on defense (less decision-making)

**Position-based substitution:**
- QB: rotate every 2-3 series — not every play (kids need continuity to execute)
- Speed position (WR/RB): can sub more freely
- Linebacker contain position: great slot for kids coming off bench — simple assignment, easy to execute without warmup

### Play Sheet Organization

**Coach D's system:**
- Wristbands with plays printed — 1 play per wristband slot, or 4 per page printout
- Each play has a simple number or name — not a complex NFL code
- Plays organized by: base formation first, misdirection plays second, short-yardage/goal-line third
- **Have only 5-6 plays total at this age level** — master a few rather than barely know many

**Calling plays:**
- Call the play number, not the full description: "We're running Play 3"
- Kids look at wrist → know their assignment → no group explanation needed
- Practice the exact words you'll use in games during practice — same vocabulary

**Field awareness reminders (Coach D):**
The three things every kid should know before every snap:
1. **Where is the first down marker?**
2. **Where is the end zone?**
3. **Where is the sideline (out of bounds)?**

Reinforce this every time you huddle or call a play. Kids routinely step out with an open field ahead of them — prevent this by making it a pre-snap ritual.
